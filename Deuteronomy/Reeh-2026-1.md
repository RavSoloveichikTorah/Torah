לא תבשל גדי‎ … עשר תעשר. מָה עִנְיַן זֶה אֵצֶל זֶה? אָמַר לָהֶם הַקָּבָּ"ה לְיִשְׂרָאֵל: אַל תִּגְרְמוּ לִי לְבַשֵּׁל גְּדָיִים שֶׁל תְּבוּאָה עַד שֶׁהֵן בִּמְעֵי אִמּוֹתֵיהֶן, שֶׁאִם אֵין אַתֶּם מְעַשְּׂרִים מַעַשְׂרוֹת כָּרָאוּי כְּשֶׁהוּא סָמוּךְ לְהִתְבַּשֵּׁל אֲנִי מוֹצִיא רוּחַ קָדִים וְהִיא מְשַׁדַּפְתָּן, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר (מלכים ב י"ט) "וּשְׁדֵפָה לִפְנֵי קָמָה", וְכֵן לְעִנְיַן בִּכּוּרִים (פסיק' ר'; תנחומא(

THOU SHALT TRULY TITHE [ALL THE INCREASE OF THY SEED] — What has this matter to do with that? (Why are they placed in juxtaposition)? But the Holy One, blessed be He, says in effect to Israel: “Do not compel Me to blast by heat the tender kernels of the grain, whilst they are yet in their mother’s womb (i.e. in the husks), for if you do not tithe your products as is proper, when they are near ripening I shall bring forth the east wind and it will blast them”, as it is said, (2 Kings 19:26) “[Therefore … they were] as the corn blasted before it be grown up” (Midrash Tanchuma, Re'eh 17). A similar reason may be given for בכורים (for its juxtaposition to לא תבשל גדי (Exodus 34:26).

We have a rule of דורשים סמוכים, we derive special meaning from the juxtaposition of verses and topics in the Torah (Yevamos 4a, Berachos 10a). Even Rabbi Yehuda, who disagrees with this rule regarding the first four Books of the Torah, agrees that we do so in the Book of Deuteronomy. Rashi juxtaposes the end of the verse about cooking the kid in mother’s milk with the beginning of the verse about tithing, and quotes the Midrash Tanchuma that there is a connection between famine and tithing. The success of our harvests depends on our tithing properly. 

The commandment of tithing includes an aspect of charity, צדקה. The commandment of charity includes an obligation that when we are blessed by Gd with the realization and fulfillment of our needs, we raise up those less fortunate and improve their lives as well. Indeed, the Torah refers to the poor as אחיך ענייך, your brother, your destitute. If we have a successful harvest, we are commanded to be cognizant of those that were not so blessed. For how can we rejoice while our brother, our neighbor sits on the sidelines wondering how he and his family will make it through the long winter without adequate provisions? Indeed, in each of the successive sections through the conclusion of the Parasha, the Torah stresses the obligation to make sure those less fortunate, the poor, the widow, the orphan, the Levite, are included in our celebration.

While this is indeed a noble concept, what did the Tanchuma see in these verses to link the idea of לא תבשל גדי בחלב אמו with famine that would result if the less fortunate among the people were not provided for? What was the source for this exegesis?

There are 5 different topics introduced with the commandment of yearly tithing: 

1)	The Torah commands us to provide for the poor and we will be blessed by Gd for doing so. The Torah then adds a curious justification, that there will always be poor people therefore Gd commanded us saying open your hand to your brother, to the poor and to the destitute in your land (Deuteronomy 15:11). The Torah is stressing the connection between the blessings we receive and the way we treat others. 

2)	The types of tithes that vary depending on the year in the 7-year Shmita cycle. While מעשר ראשון, the tithe given to the Levites is always obligatory, in the third and sixth year we replace the second tithe, מעשר שני, that is brought to Jerusalem in the first, second, fourth and fifth years with the tithe given to the poor, מעשר עני. At a fundamental level, each tithe includes an aspect charity. 

3)	The laws of Shmita regarding the forgiveness of loans. We are commanded to override our base instinct to refrain from lending money to the poor as we approach the Shmita year, when all loans are automatically cancelled. As there will always be poor people to support, we must overcome our inherent selfish nature and lend money to those in need regardless of the approaching Shmita and cancelation said loans.

4)	The Torah includes the obligation to properly treat the slave who was sold by the Rabbinical Court because he stole out of destitution and lacked the resources to make restitution. includes the obligation to provide gifts to the Jewish servant when he is released after his term of servitude is complete. הענק תעניק לו מצאנך ומגרנך ומיקבך אשר ברכך השם אלקיך תתן לו (דברים טו:יד). These gifts come from your sheep, grain and wine. Why do we focus on these items? Presumably the servant has played a role in the care and growing of these items that enriched his master. It is appropriate that his work be rewarded with fruits of his labor. The master must recognize the servant’s efforts on his behalf and willingly reward him as an expression of thanks and appreciation for that work. During the time the servant is working, there are obligations on the master to support his family. Again, a clear underlying thread of charity permeates this commandment.  It is also interesting to note that we are enjoined from coercing the slave back into servitude. Once he has completed his term, he is to return to his previous status. Jeremiah (Chapter 34) describes how Gd commanded the people to free their slaves which they initially did, only to immediately repossess and coerce them back into servitude, in clear violation of the Torah Commandments. This was among the final sins that sealed the fate of the destruction of the first Temple. Gd said because you did not listen to Me to set the slaves, your brothers, free, therefore you will suffer the sword, pestilence, and famine; and I will make you a horror to all the kingdoms of the earth. You who have been enjoined from cooking the kid in the mother’s milk must be attuned to the needs of your brethren who may have been sold into servitude. If you are incapable of feeling for them, you are incapable of being considered a holy nation and the Chosen People.

5)	The obligation to appear before Gd three times a year, at Passover, Shavuot and Sukkot, מצוות עליה לרגל. At the core is the obligation to be happy, שמחה, but there also is a requirement that we make others happy as well at these times. Specifically, the Torah commands us to make sure the Levite, the widow and orphan are all included in our celebration as we appear before Gd three times yearly. As Maimonides says (Hilchos Yom Tov 6:17-18), one who seals his doors and does not include the poor and less fortunate in his celebration of a festival does not enjoy the joy of the holiday, but rather the joy of his own gluttony.  In addition to the meaning attributed to the festivals based on their role in the Exodus from Egypt, the festivals also play a role in the agricultural cycle. Each holiday includes a celebration of some aspect of the agricultural cycle, Passover with the bringing of the קרבן העומר, Shavuot with the bringing of the קרבן שתי הלחם and Sukkot with the conclusion of bringing בכורים and obligation to recite the formula of ארמי אובד אבי. These occasions are characterized by שמחה, a special feeling of joy, at having a successful harvest and the feeling of security that we are well provided for till the next growing season arrives. The Torah stresses the requirement to ensure that those less fortunate are included in our times of joy, and they also can feel the special שמחה of the holiday as we do. 


Shmita is a realization that whatever we have is from Gd and belongs to Him. We are just custodians of those resources. Our willingness to cancel loans we made in the 6 previous years, along with our adherence to leaving the land fallow and allowing everyone to take from the produce the field naturally produces are admissions on our part that all belongs to Gd and He can do as He sees fit. Acknowledgement of this that and restricting ourselves from claiming its all mine, is the expression of faith in Gd and a reason for the special sanctity of the Jewish people and a reaffirmation of the selection Gd made when He chose us at the time of the Exodus. If we don’t adhere to these laws, then everything we have can and will be taken away. If we don’t tithe, initially we will lose the 90% we were supposed to have and be left with the 10% we were supposed to give away. And if we still don’t get the message, that 10% will be taken away as well. 

The Talmud (Kidushin 20a) discusses possible reasons that lead to a Jew being sold as a slave. It quotes Rabbi Yosi Ben Rabbi Chanina that one who violates the sanctity and laws associated with Shmita year produce will suffer a series of financial losses that ultimately lead to the sale of his daughter and himself as a slave, possibly to non-Jewish owners. The punishment associated with violating aspects of Shmita laws are stark.

The Torah addresses the issues of the Shmita year in terms of our concern that we will not be repaid, or we will not have sufficient food if we allow others to take from our fields during the Shmita year. The Torah tells us to consider those that are in need. What are they thinking about during the same time period? They realize that the approaching Shmita will mean limited financial opportunities for them. If the benefactors they depend on will be reluctant to help them because of their selfish needs, how will they possibly make ends meet? If we observe the financial and agricultural laws of Shmita and ensure that the concerns and worries of the less fortunate are addressed regardless of the proximity to the Shmita year, we will be blessed and provided for. If we don’t observe the laws of Shmita, whether the law pertaining to agriculture or to financial issues, we will be put in the situation of those less fortunate and experience their plight firsthand.

Where did Tanchuma find this linkage between these sections? Perhaps the answer is in Pirkei Avot (6:8-9):

שִׁבְעָה מִינֵי פֻרְעָנֻיּוֹת בָּאִין לָעוֹלָם עַל שִׁבְעָה גוּפֵי עֲבֵרָה. מִקְצָתָן מְעַשְּׂרִין וּמִקְצָתָן אֵינָן מְעַשְּׂרִין, רָעָב שֶׁל בַּצֹּרֶת בָּאָה, מִקְצָתָן רְעֵבִים וּמִקְצָתָן שְׂבֵעִים. גָּמְרוּ שֶׁלֹּא לְעַשֵּׂר, רָעָב שֶׁל מְהוּמָה וְשֶׁל בַּצֹּרֶת בָּאָה. וְשֶׁלֹּא לִטֹּל אֶת הַחַלָּה, רָעָב שֶׁל כְּלָיָה בָאָה. דֶּבֶר בָּא לָעוֹלָם עַל מִיתוֹת הָאֲמוּרוֹת בַּתּוֹרָה שֶׁלֹּא נִמְסְרוּ לְבֵית דִּין, וְעַל פֵּרוֹת שְׁבִיעִית. חֶרֶב בָּאָה לָעוֹלָם עַל עִנּוּי הַדִּין, וְעַל עִוּוּת הַדִּין, וְעַל הַמּוֹרִים בַּתּוֹרָה שֶׁלֹּא כַהֲלָכָה:

Seven kinds of punishment come to the world for seven categories of transgression: When some of them tithe and others do not, a famine from drought comes some go hungry, and others are satisfied. When they have all decided not to give tithes, a famine from tumult and drought comes; [When they have, in addition, decided] not to set apart the dough-offering, an all-consuming famine comes. Pestilence comes to the world for sins punishable by death according to the Torah, but which have not been referred to the court, and for neglect of the law regarding the fruits of the sabbatical year. The sword comes to the world for the delay of judgment, and for the perversion of judgment, and because of those who teach the Torah not in accordance with the accepted law.

חַיָּה רָעָה בָאָה לָעוֹלָם עַל שְׁבוּעַת שָׁוְא, וְעַל חִלּוּל הַשֵּׁם. גָּלוּת בָּאָה לָעוֹלָם עַל עוֹבְדֵי עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה, וְעַל גִלּוּי עֲרָיוֹת, וְעַל שְׁפִיכוּת דָּמִים, וְעַל הַשְׁמָטַת הָאָרֶץ. בְּאַרְבָּעָה פְרָקִים הַדֶּבֶר מִתְרַבֶּה, בָּרְבִיעִית, וּבַשְּׁבִיעִית, וּבְמוֹצָאֵי שְׁבִיעִית, וּבְמוֹצָאֵי הֶחָג שֶׁבְּכָל שָׁנָה וְשָׁנָה. בָּרְבִיעִית, מִפְּנֵי מַעְשַׂר עָנִי שֶׁבַּשְּׁלִישִׁית. בַּשְּׁבִיעִית, מִפְּנֵי מַעְשַׂר עָנִי שֶׁבַּשִּׁשִּׁית. וּבְמוֹצָאֵי שְׁבִיעִית, מִפְּנֵי פֵרוֹת שְׁבִיעִית. וּבְמוֹצָאֵי הֶחָג שֶׁבְּכָל שָׁנָה וְשָׁנָה, מִפְּנֵי גֶזֶל מַתְּנוֹת עֲנִיִּים:

Wild beasts come to the world for swearing in vain, and for the profanation of the Name. Exile comes to the world for idolatry, for sexual sins and for bloodshed, and for [transgressing the commandment of] the [year of the] release of the land. At four times pestilence increases: in the fourth year, in the seventh year and at the conclusion of the seventh year, and at the conclusion of the Feast [of Tabernacles] in every year. In the fourth year, on account of the tithe of the poor which is due in the third year. In the seventh year, on account of the tithe of the poor which is due in the sixth year; At the conclusion of the seventh year, on account of the produce of the seventh year; And at the conclusion of the Feast [of Tabernacles] in every year, for robbing the gifts to the poor.

These Mishnayot focus on the causes for famine, war and disease in the Jewish world. They include the dereliction of tithing and misusing the fruits of the seventh year as well as ignoring the Shmita laws related to the land. Although they do not explicitly mention שמיטת כספים, forgiving loans, it is included in the other laws od Shmita. Not giving the tithe of the poor, מעשר עני, in the third and seventh year are explicitly called out. The end of the Mishna includes מוצאי חג הסוכות, after the Sukkot festival, because refusing to give the tithe of the poor is equated with stealing the gifts intended for the poor, מִפְּנֵי גֶזֶל מַתְּנוֹת עֲנִיִּים. Misappropriating Shmita year produce is clearly called out as a cause for exile, as ther can be no greater exile than when one sold into servitude, 
as Rabbi Yosi Ben Rabbi Chanina said.

Indeed, the prophets called out this sinful behavior on the part of the people. Malachi (3:1)) asked the people to test Gd by tithing and seeing if they would be rewarded with more where they were fearful of having less. Jeremiah called out their sin with the way they treated their Hebrew slaves. And our Rabbis tell us that the first exile lasted 70 years in accordance with the number of Shmitot the people violated after entering the land. Isaiah called out their perversion of the festivals, which were supposed to be times of joy focused on the intimate relationship between Gd and his people and by the charity to be given to the less fortunate, by turning them into periods Gd loathed. The result was famine, disease and destruction.

The success of the growing season depends on Gd’s providing adequate or even abundant rainfall so the seeds we plant and the grain we sow will grow properly.
The famine and the subsequent disease and destruction were foretold and forewarned by the verse that prohibited cooking the kid in its mother’s milk as quoted by Rashi.

Why was such a drastic punishment necessary? Because the Torah tells us (Deuteronomy 14:21)
לֹ֣א תֹאכְל֣וּ כׇל־נְ֠בֵלָ֠ה לַגֵּ֨ר אֲשֶׁר־בִּשְׁעָרֶ֜יךָ תִּתְּנֶ֣נָּה וַאֲכָלָ֗הּ א֤וֹ מָכֹר֙ לְנׇכְרִ֔י כִּ֣י עַ֤ם קָדוֹשׁ֙ אַתָּ֔ה להשם אֱלֹהֶ֑יךָ לֹֽא־תְבַשֵּׁ֥ל גְּדִ֖י בַּחֲלֵ֥ב אִמּֽוֹ׃ 

You shall not eat anything that has died a natural death; give it to the stranger in your community to eat, or you may sell it to a foreigner. For you are a people consecrated to your God. You shall not boil a kid in its mother’s milk.

The Jew is endowed with sanctity that forms our bond With Gd. As it says (Deuteronomy 14:2): 
כִּ֣י עַ֤ם קָדוֹשׁ֙ אַתָּ֔ה להשם אֱלֹהֶ֑יךָ וּבְךָ֞ בָּחַ֣ר השם לִֽהְי֥וֹת לוֹ֙ לְעַ֣ם סְגֻלָּ֔ה מִכֹּל֙ הָֽעַמִּ֔ים אֲשֶׁ֖ר עַל־פְּנֵ֥י הָאֲדָמָֽה׃ 

For you are a people consecrated to your God: your God chose you from among all other peoples on earth to be a treasured people.

Maintaining our sanctity is sine qua non for our selection by God. That sanctity is based on our observance of core principles, including refraining from consuming non-kosher animals as well as providing for those in need. The Torah tells us that there will always be poor living in the land. The pre-requisite for remaining in the land and enjoying its bountiful gifts is to retain our sanctity, the reason for our initial selection, through the food we eat and by caring for the less fortunate. The formula for success is clear. Unfortunately, the inverse applies to those who do not follow that prescription, as described in מסכת אבות.
