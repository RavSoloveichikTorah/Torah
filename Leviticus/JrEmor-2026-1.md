In a non-leap year, Parshas Emor coincides with the fourth chapter of Tractate Avot. As there are no coincidences in Jewish life, 
what is the connection between them? Often, there is a root word or concept that appears more frequently in a particular Parsha than others. 
In Emor we find the root word Chilul appears many times in reference to the Kohanim as well as to the sacrifices we are obligated to bring to 
the Temple where the Priests perform the service on our behalf. The priest must make sure not to be Mchalel, desecrate himself, for example 
he is enjoined from marrying women that would be permissible to the rest of the Jewish community. The people are obligated to sanctify him 
because he was selected to offer sacrifices on their behalf. The high priest has even more restrictions when it comes to Chilul Kehuna. 
He must always remain in the Temple vicinity lest he be Mchalel his Kehuna Gedolah. Where a regular priest may defile himself in attending to the 
funeral of his 7 direct relatives, the Kohen Gadol may not attend any funeral, even for his parents. I would like to explore the significance 
of the word Chilul as well as the underlying idea it conveys.

We usually interpret the word L’Chalel as to desecrate. For example, we refer to Chilul Shabbat or Chilul Hashem as desecrating the 
Sabbath or Kvayachol the name of Gd. We also find the concept of Chilul when we want to redeem an item that has Hekdesh sanctity 
associated with it. We transfer the sanctity of the object to something else, and we can use the original item for our own purposes. 
The classic example is fruits of Maaser Shayni, the tithe we are obligated to bring to Jerusalem in years 1,2,4,5 of the 7-year Shmita cycle. 
If we have an abundant harvest and cannot transport all of it to Jerusalem, we can redeem the fruits for money which we then spend in 
Jerusalem. The Chilul removes the sanctity of the object and renders it Chol or mundane, usable outside Jerusalem. 
Indeed, the prohibitions against Chilul Shabbat and Chilul Hashem derive from our obligation to treat Hashem and the Sabbath 
with special reverence. Doing anything that negates or diminishes that reverence or renders them mundane, Chol, is considered a grave sin, 
punishable by death under certain conditions. 

Interestingly, we also find the word Kadosh, sanctity, appearing throughout the Parsha. Kedusha and Chilul are opposites. 
They cannot co-exist in the same object.  Nor can they co-exist in the same person. If he is deserving, we exalt and honor the priest, 
V’Kidashto. If he desecrates the people’s trust and his position, he causes Chilul Kehuna, desecration of the Priesthood, 
which ultimately leads to Chilul Hashem, desecration of Gd’s name. The priest is the link between the people and Gd. 
If he acts in a way that demonstrates a mundane attitude towards Gd by trivializing his Kehuna, he cannot perform his appointed tasks. 
If Gd’s selected representatives act in a way that impugns the priesthood, ultimately it reflects poorly on Gd as well. 
The priest will forfeit any respect the people had for him. An individual cannot be self-contradictory, sanctified and revered 
while acting like the rest of society. A prince does not behave as a commoner. People will perceive him by his actions, for good or bad. 

However, there is more to the priest than the physical service performed in the Temple. The Torah and prophets tell us that the priests 
also served as the judges and teachers. They needed to act in a transparent way that was beyond reproach. For if they themselves were 
immoral or did not respect the Torah laws, whether between Man and Gd or Man and Man, the result would be not only a lack of respect 
and appreciation of the priests themselves, but it would reflect poorly on the Torah and Gd. The ultimate desecration occurs if a Priest 
or a judge or teacher twists the law and acts improperly. Immoral leaders set a slippery slope the rest of society will fall on.

Why do the actions of the Priests, judges and teachers reflect so heavily on the honor of Gd, Kedushat Hashem? Why are they so special? 
In Parshat Ki Teitze we are told:

וְכִֽי־יִהְיֶ֣ה בְאִ֗ישׁ חֵ֛טְא מִשְׁפַּט־מָ֖וֶת וְהוּמָ֑ת וְתָלִ֥יתָ אֹת֖וֹ עַל־עֵֽץ׃ לֹא־תָלִ֨ין נִבְלָת֜וֹ עַל־הָעֵ֗ץ כִּֽי־קָב֤וֹר תִּקְבְּרֶ֙נּוּ֙ בַּיּ֣וֹם הַה֔וּא כִּֽי־קִלְלַ֥ת אֱלֹהִ֖ים תָּל֑וּי וְלֹ֤א תְטַמֵּא֙ אֶת־אַדְמָ֣תְךָ֔ אֲשֶׁר֙ יְהֹוָ֣ה אֱלֹהֶ֔יךָ נֹתֵ֥ן לְךָ֖ נַחֲלָֽה׃
רש״י

כי קללת אלהים תלוי. זִלְזוּלוֹ שֶׁל מֶלֶךְ הוּא, שֶׁאָדָם עָשׂוּי בִּדְמוּת דְּיוֹקָנוֹ, וְיִשְׂרָאֵל הֵם בָּנָיו; מָשָׁל לִשְׁנֵי אַחִים תְּאוֹמִים שֶׁהָיוּ דּוֹמִים זֶה לָזֶה, אֶחָד נַעֲשָׂה מֶלֶךְ וְאֶחָד נִתְפַּס לְלִסְטִיּוּת וְנִתְלָה, כָּל הָרוֹאֶה אוֹתוֹ אוֹמֵר הַמֶּלֶךְ תָּלוּי.

We are told in the Book of Genesis that man was created in the image of Gd:
בראשית  א:כז
וַיִּבְרָ֨א אֱלֹהִ֤ים ׀ אֶת־הָֽאָדָם֙ בְּצַלְמֹ֔ו בְּצֶ֥לֶם אֱלֹהִ֖ים בָּרָ֣א אֹתֹ֑ו זָכָ֥ר וּנְקֵבָ֖ה בָּרָ֥א אֹתָֽם׃
בראשית ט:ו
שֹׁפֵךְ֙ דַּ֣ם הָֽאָדָ֔ם בָּֽאָדָ֖ם דָּמֹ֣ו יִשָּׁפֵ֑ךְ כִּ֚י בְּצֶ֣לֶם אֱלֹהִ֔ים עָשָׂ֖ה אֶת־הָאָדָֽם׃

When people see a criminal, who bears a strong resemblance to his Creator, swinging from a tree by a noose around his neck, they may think that 
it is the Creator, Kvayachol, hanging, and not his look-alike relative. When man sins, he reflects poorly on Gd, his Creator. 
King David describes how man can reach near Godly heights, reflective of his Creator. He also can descend to the lowest depths, 
considered lower than the mosquito and as irrelevant as dust and ashes:
תהילים ה-ז
מָה־אֱנ֥וֹשׁ כִּֽי־תִזְכְּרֶ֑נּוּ וּבֶן־אָ֝דָ֗ם כִּ֣י תִפְקְדֶֽנּוּ׃ וַתְּחַסְּרֵ֣הוּ מְּ֭עַט מֵאֱלֹהִ֑ים וְכָב֖וֹד וְהָדָ֣ר תְּעַטְּרֵֽהוּ׃ תַּ֭מְשִׁילֵהוּ בְּמַעֲשֵׂ֣י יָדֶ֑יךָ כֹּ֝֗ל שַׁ֣תָּה תַֽחַת־רַגְלָֽיו׃

When man acts properly, and sanctifies Gd’s name, he reflects well on his Creator, Who is happy that man was created in His image. 
However, if he desecrates Gd’s name by acting immorally, he is considered useless, like the dust and ashes of the earth.

The Talmud tells us:
נדה לא:א
תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: שְׁלֹשָׁה שׁוּתָּפִין יֵשׁ בָּאָדָם — הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא, וְאָבִיו, וְאִמּוֹ.

 There are 3 partners in creating a human being: father, mother and Gd. When an individual sins, his actions reflect on his parents and Gd. 
 When he is exposed for all to see in his sinful state, observers can easily confuse him with his Creator, in whose image he was created. 
 When the sinful individual is a Priest, judge, teacher, rabbi, community leader, the associated Chilul Hashem is magnified even more. 
 Such individuals must always be mindful of their actions. The slightest error can have terrible consequences. 

The Torah does not tell us when Rebecca died. We have a hint as to her passing when the Torah tells us about the death of 
Rebecca’s nursemaid, Devorah. Rashi quotes the Midrash that Gd did not want to mention her passing in the Torah, lest people curse 
the womb that brought Esau into the world. Esau’s immoral and wicked life reflected on his mother. (See Rashi Genesis 35:8 ד״ה תחת האלון.)

The Talmud derives the obligation to sanctify the name of Hashem when it is called for from the verse:
ויקרא כב:לב
וְלֹ֤א תְחַלְּלוּ֙ אֶת־שֵׁ֣ם קׇדְשִׁ֔י וְנִ֨קְדַּשְׁתִּ֔י בְּת֖וֹךְ בְּנֵ֣י יִשְׂרָאֵ֑ל אֲנִ֥י יְהֹוָ֖ה מְקַדִּשְׁכֶֽם׃ 

Desecration and sanctification are two sides of a coin. They cannot coexist. Where Kiddush Hashem is the highest level a Jew can attain
by sacrificing his life for Gd when called upon, the stain and punishment associated with Chilul Hashem is the gravest. Maimonides says 
that one who seeks to atone for a sin associated with Chilul Hashem must undergo various punishments, including afflictions and ultimately 
death to remove the sin:
רמב״ם הלכות תשובה א:ד 
אֲבָל הַמְחַלֵּל אֶת הַשֵּׁם אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁעָשָׂה תְּשׁוּבָה וְהִגִּיעַ יוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים וְהוּא עוֹמֵד בִּתְשׁוּבָתוֹ וּבָאוּ עָלָיו יִסּוּרִין אֵינוֹ מִתְכַּפֵּר לוֹ כַּפָּרָה גְּמוּרָה עַד שֶׁיָּמוּת. אֶלָּא תְּשׁוּבָה יוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים וְיִסּוּרִין שְׁלָשְׁתָּן תּוֹלִין וּמִיתָה מְכַפֶּרֶת שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר (ישעיה כב יד) "וְנִגְלָה בְאָזְנָי ה' צְבָאוֹת" וְגוֹ' (ישעיה כב יד) "אִם יְכֻפַּר הֶעָוֹן הַזֶּה לָכֶם עַד תְּמֻתוּן":


Tractate Avot focuses on the interpersonal laws and how people, especially rabbis and judges should treat their fellow human beings. 
Chazal were very mindful that people in positions of Torah and community responsibility act properly, avoiding even the hint of impropriety. 
The Talmud tells us (Yuma 86a) that Rav would not buy meat from a butcher on credit, lest people think he was stealing, 
causing a Chilul Hashem. Clearly Rav would never act in this way, but even the possibility of someone misconstruing his actions 
and causing a desecration of Gd’s name because of his important position, was enough to make Rav sensitive to the possibility.

The fourth chapter in Avot focuses on the same ideas: Desecration and Sanctification of the name of Gd, by acting appropriately 
in interpersonal matters, בין אדם לחברו, or between man and Gd, בין אדם למקום. For example:

אֵיזֶהוּ מְכֻבָּד, הַמְכַבֵּד אֶת הַבְּרִיּוֹת, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר (שמואל א ב) כִּי מְכַבְּדַי אֲכַבֵּד וּבֹזַי יֵקָלּוּ: 
אבות ד:ב
רַבִּי לְוִיטָס אִישׁ יַבְנֶה אוֹמֵר, מְאֹד מְאֹד הֱוֵי שְׁפַל רוּחַ, שֶׁתִּקְוַת אֱנוֹשׁ רִמָּה. רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן בְּרוֹקָא אוֹמֵר, כָּל הַמְחַלֵּל שֵׁם שָׁמַיִם בַּסֵּתֶר, נִפְרָעִין מִמֶּנּוּ בְגָלוּי. אֶחָד שׁוֹגֵג וְאֶחָד מֵזִיד בְּחִלּוּל הַשֵּׁם:

רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן שַׁמּוּעַ אוֹמֵר, יְהִי כְבוֹד תַּלְמִידְךָ חָבִיב עָלֶיךָ כְּשֶׁלְּךָ, וּכְבוֹד חֲבֵרְךָ כְּמוֹרָא רַבְּךָ, וּמוֹרָא רַבְּךָ כְּמוֹרָא שָׁמָיִם: 

Chazal recognized that Kavod HaBriyot, respect for other people and Gd’s creation, is another indicator of desecration or 
sanctification of Gd’s name. If a scholar, teacher, judge treats other people fairly and with dignity, people will praise him 
as well as Gd and His Torah that taught him such exemplary character traits, resulting in a true sanctification of Gd’s name.
If one acts improperly and immorally, people will reject the Teacher and Teachings, Hashem and His Torah, causing a terrible 
desecration of Gd’s name.
 אבות ג:יד
הוּא הָיָה אוֹמֵר, חָבִיב אָדָם שֶׁנִּבְרָא בְצֶלֶם. חִבָּה יְתֵרָה נוֹדַעַת לוֹ שֶׁנִּבְרָא בְצֶלֶם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר כִּי בְּצֶלֶם אֱלֹהִים עָשָׂה אֶת הָאָדָם.
